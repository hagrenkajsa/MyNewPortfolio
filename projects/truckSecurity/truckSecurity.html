Truck Driver Security

A case study exploring a security system concept designed to prevent theft and support truck drivers.

Project Overview

Project Background

25% of all truck drivers have experienced theft at least once. 140 thefts are reported every day, with an average daily loss of EUR 800,000 in the EMEA region.

Most truck drivers have little to no support when it comes to preventing theft or receiving help during a theft. One way to reduce the risk is by using a security system. Currently, Volvo offers only limited security services to its customers and now the company wants to explore this area further.

Task

Explore a security system that can prevent theft and support the driver during an ongoing theft.

The solution should be a concept of a holistic security system that supports truck drivers during criminal incidents, with a focus on security from the driver’s perspective and business from the customer’s perspective, which in Volvo’s case, primarily refers to fleet managers.

Research Questions

  • RQ1: What are the security-related pain points from a driver’s perspective?

  • RQ2: How can these pain points be addressed to both enhance driver security and prevent fuel and cargo theft with the help of a holistic security system?

Interview format

Semi-structured interviews through Teams meetings.

The participants had the option to be interviewed either in person or via Microsoft Teams. All of them chose to be interviewed online for various reasons. Each interview lasted approximately 30–40 minutes and followed a semi-structured interview guide to ensure consistency while allowing flexibility in the discussions. Because security concerns are a complex topic, the semi-structured format made it possible to adapt questions as needed while still covering the most important themes. The interview guide contained 22 main questions, but I occasionally skipped or added questions depending on the participants’ responses. All interviews were recorded and transcribed using the built-in tools in Microsoft Teams.

Literature Review

General Research on Theft in the Trucking Industry

To gain a broad understanding of the problem area, I conducted a literature review focusing on various aspects of theft within the trucking industry. I examined different locations, target products, thief behaviors and took a closer look at the underlying elements of these crimes.

Define

Interview Analysis

Thematic analysis

To analyze and present the findings from the interviews, I conducted a thematic analysis to highlight insights across different categories related to driver security. To make the analysis more efficient, I used an online tool called Delve, which helps you structure qualitative data by assigning categories to the interview transcripts and organizing them in a hierarchical way.

Interview findings

Theft is a major concern among the drivers and they expressed shared similar ideas about the type of support they would like.

I received many interesting responses and gained a solid understanding of the drivers’ various problems, behaviors and needs. Some of the most noteworthy findings are presented in the list below. Please note that this summary does not include all insights gathered from the interviews.

  • All respondents were concerned about security, although some more than others.

  • Those who were most worried about security were mainly concerned about their own safety rather than the safety of the cargo or the truck.

  • Those who were less worried about their own safety were more concerned about the security of the cargo or the truck.

  • Two respondents expressed concerns about the vulnerability of women in the industry.

  • All respondents, except one, expressed at least some interest in a security system.

  • When asked what kind of technology or solution they would like, most mentioned cameras, alarm sounds and door sensors for the trailers.

  • Other ideas included a panic button, alarms to alert the driver, motion sensors, lights, automatic alerts to the police, evidence collection, shell alarms, sensors over the fuel tank, alerts to nearby drivers with sound and better access to secure parking areas.

  • Most respondents expressed frustration over crime in the transport industry. Some said it disrupted the flow of work, especially due to fuel theft. A few mentioned waking up to find their fuel stolen, which caused delays in their schedule.

  • Respondents were motivated both by a desire to feel safe and a hope that thieves would get caught.

How Might We - Questions

How might we questions was defined.

To make the problem even more tangible, I formulated multiple HMW questions for each JTBD statement. The main purpose of these is to use them in a workshop during the ideate phase, which is presented in the next section.

Jobs-To-Be-Done

To define the problem into something tangible, I defined five Jobs-To-Be-Done (JTBD) statements.

The statements were based on findings from the thematic analysis and from the literature review of reports on the issue of truck-related crime. The purpose of these statements is to highlight the underlying motivations, desires and constraints behind several problems. In this case, they are meant to reflect the findings from my research. The statements are presented in the tables below.

Insight 1

Job

I want to be able to sleep in the truck or leave it for a while without worrying about being robbed.

Motivation

I want to feel more free and independent in my work.

Desired outcome

My work would be less stressful and more flexible.

Constraints

I have to worry if I do not park in a safe place, and safe parking spots are often expensive and hard to find.

Insight 2

Job

I want to be aware of what is happening if I am being robbed.

Motivation

I want to know whether it is just a kid messing around or a serious threat, like four armed men.

Desired outcome

That would help me make better decisions in that situation.

Constraints

I currently have no tools or systems that give me an overview of the situation.

Insight 3

Job

I want to make sure that I stay safe in any situation.

Motivation

I can’t be replaced, but the cargo can.

Desired outcome

I want to make it home safely at the end of the day.

Constraints

But sometimes, thieves may use violence or threats to get access to the trailer.

Insight 4

Job

I do not want my truck to be a target for thieves.

Motivation

I want other vehicles to seem like more attractive targets than mine.

Desired outcome

So they choose to rob them instead of me.

Constraints

It is hard to make my truck look unappealing when I am carrying high-value cargo.

Insight 5

Job

I want the thieves to get caught if they try to rob me.

Motivation

Because they deserve to face consequences.

Desired outcome

They will not do it again, and I will get my cargo back.

Constraints

They are usually gone before I even notice the theft.

As mentioned earlier, I defined five JTBD statements. For each statement, I prepared a set of corresponding HMW (How Might We) questions. Finalize ideas

Combining workshop ideas with the Theft Journey to create a holistic solution

The workshop generated several valuable ideas for improving truck driver security, particularly concerning fuel and cargo theft. To combine and refine these ideas into a holistic and feasible security system, I used the Theft Journey as a guiding framework, as it highlighted opportunities to both support the driver and deter the thief. For each opportunity identified in the journey, I matched relevant ideas from the workshop to see how they could contribute. This process helped me develop a comprehensive security system concept that simultaneously empowers the driver and prevents theft.

Prototype

System description

To visualize the concepts, a system description was developed and serves as the prototype for this project

To create this system description, I had to have a close dialog with different stakeholders to make sure the concept was inside the frame of Volvo’s feasibility.

The security system is built around two states: Proactive Action and Reactive Action. The purpose of the proactive action is to detect an incident. When an incident is detected, it triggers the reactive actions. The reason for this setup is to avoid having the full system active all the time, which helps reduce energy consumption.

Conclusion

Research Conclusions

The research demonstrates that a holistic security system can address drivers’ security concerns and offer a promising business case for Volvo.

In conclusion, this project explored the issue of cargo and fuel theft in the trucking industry and answered two research questions:

  • RQ1: What are the security-related pain points from a driver’s perspective?

  • RQ2: How can these pain points be addressed to both enhance driver security and prevent fuel and cargo theft with the help of a holistic security system?

To answer RQ1, the project found that truck drivers have clear security concerns. Some worry more about their personal safety, while others focus on protecting the truck and its cargo. However, most drivers expressed similar needs and wanted features such as cameras, alarm sounds, door sensors, evidence collection tools, and quick access to the police.

The prototype developed in this project addresses RQ2. It provides a solution to the problems identified in RQ1 by focusing on a holistic security system designed to support the driver and stop thieves from completing a theft.

To evaluate the concept from a business perspective, six hypotheses were tested. Five were supported, and one was not. The results indicate that the idea could represent a strong business opportunity because it appears both feasible to build and in demand on the market.

The research also suggests that Volvo could offer this security system as an additional service. This would not only meet drivers’ needs but could also reduce theft risk, lower insurance costs, and increase uptime which leads to higher profits for fleet managers. This was the main goal of the business idea.

From Volvo’s perspective, there is a significant opportunity. The technology already exists, there is clear market demand, and people within Volvo Group’s finance and services departments believe that a system like this could become a valuable business opportunity.
